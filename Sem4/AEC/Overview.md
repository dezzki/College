# Unit 1: Global Environmental Issues and Policies

## Topic 1: Climate Change, Global Warming, Acid Rain, and Ozone Depletion

### Overview

This topic explores large-scale anthropogenic impacts on the global environment, focusing on the alteration of the earth's atmospheric chemistry and its widespread consequences.

### Definitions

- 📝 **Global Warming**: The rapid increase in the earth's surface temperature primarily due to the trapping of heat by greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide.
- 📝 **Acid Rain**: The deposition of highly corrosive acidic pollutants (like sulfuric acid and nitric acid), formed when sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides react with water in the atmosphere.
- 📝 **Ozone Layer Depletion**: The thinning of the protective ozone layer (located 20 to 50 km above the earth) caused by pollutants, significantly increasing respiratory ailments and health hazards.

### Core Concepts

- **Greenhouse Effect**: Approximately 75% of solar energy is absorbed by the earth, while the rest radiates back; greenhouse gases trap this radiating heat, leading to global warming.
- **Impacts of Climate Change**: Shifts in global climate result in melting polar ice caps, a rise in sea levels, coastal flooding, and severe disruption to global agriculture and water resources. Food and water shortages can lead to starvation, malnutrition, and the displacement of people, creating "environmental refugees".
- **Acid Deposition**: Occurs as wet deposition (rain, fog, snow) or dry deposition (particles and gases) that damage flora, fauna, and property.

Not applicable.

### Examples

- Reduced agricultural yield and localized food shortages due to altered rainfall patterns and extreme weather conditions.

### PYQ Importance

- ⭐ **Frequently Asked**: The main causes of global warming and climate change, and the critical impacts of climate change on agriculture and water resources.

### Exam Focus

- 📌 **Must Memorize**: The specific impacts of climate change on agriculture, human health, and global economy.
- ⚠️ **Commonly Confused Concept**: Distinguishing between the impacts of global warming (heat trapping) and ozone depletion (increased UV radiation exposure).

## Topic 2: International Agreements and Programmes

### Overview

This topic covers the global treaties formulated to tackle cross-boundary environmental crises such as greenhouse gas emissions, biodiversity loss, and wetland degradation.

### Definitions

- 📝 **Ramsar Convention**: An international treaty strictly focused on the protection of wetlands and waterfowl habitats.
- 📝 **UNFCCC**: United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, focused on addressing climate change issues.

### Core Concepts

- **Montreal Protocol vs. Kyoto Protocol**: The Montreal Protocol is aimed at phasing out Ozone Depleting Substances (like CFCs), whereas the Kyoto Protocol regulates Greenhouse Gases (GHGs) to combat climate change.
- **Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD)**: Focuses on conserving biodiversity and ensuring the sustainable use of biological resources.
- **UNCCD**: Specifically addresses desertification and land degradation issues.

### Important Formulas / Relations

Not applicable.

### Procedures / Algorithms

### PYQ Importance

- ⭐ **Frequently Asked**: Differentiating between the Montreal and Kyoto Protocols.
- ⭐ **Frequently Asked**: Matching international conventions (Ramsar, CBD, CITES, UNFCCC) with their respective objectives.

### Exam Focus

- 📌 **Must Memorize**: Montreal = CFCs/Ozone; Kyoto = GHGs/Global Warming; Ramsar = Wetlands.
- ⚠️ **Commonly Confused Concept**: Assuming CFCs were banned under the Ramsar Convention; they were banned under the Montreal Protocol.

## Topic 3: Environmental Legislation in India

### Overview

A review of the statutory laws enacted by the Government of India to protect wildlife, forests, and control pollution in water and air.

### Definitions

- 📝 **Environmental Protection Act (EPA), 1986**: An umbrella legislation enacted post-Bhopal Gas Tragedy to protect and improve the overall environment.

### Core Concepts

- **Wildlife Protection Act, 1972**: Outlines the establishment of National Parks and Sanctuaries and categorizes endangered species to prohibit their hunting/poaching.
- **Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974**: Empowers the Central and State Pollution Control Boards (CPCB/SPCB) to set standards and restrict industrial effluent discharge.
- **Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980**: Strictly prohibits the use of forest land for non-forestry purposes without central government approval.

# Unit 2: Biodiversity and Conservation

## Topic 1: Levels and Values of Biodiversity

### Overview

Biodiversity encompasses the variety of life forms on earth. This topic details the classification, measurement, and intrinsic values of biodiversity.

### Definitions

- 📝 **Alpha Diversity**: Diversity of organisms sharing the same specific community or habitat.
- 📝 **Beta & Gamma Diversity**: Beta measures diversity between different communities, while Gamma measures overall diversity across a large geographical landscape.
- 📝 **Endemic Species**: Species that have a highly localized distribution and are restricted exclusively to a specific country or region.

### Core Concepts

- **Levels of Biodiversity**: Divided into Genetic (variation within species), Species (variety of species), and Ecosystem (variation in structure and functions of ecosystems).
- **Values of Biodiversity**:
    - _Consumptive Value_: Direct use of biodiversity products like fuelwood and food.
    - _Productive Value_: Commercially usable products marketed and sold (e.g., musk, tusks, silk, wool).
    - _Ethical Value_: The inherent right of all species to exist, regardless of their direct utility to humans.

Not applicable.

### Examples

- The Dodo and a specific species of Pigeon are examples of ethically valued species that have gone extinct.

### Exam Focus

## Topic 2: Threats to Biodiversity and Ecosystems

### Overview

This section outlines the anthropogenic pressures threatening global and Indian biodiversity, leading to the current mass extinction crisis.

### Definitions

- 📝 **Invasive Species**: Introduced or foreign species that disrupt local ecosystems; however, _not all_ introduced species become invasive.
- 📝 **Keystone Species**: Species that determine the ability of a large number of other species to persist in a community (e.g., fig, peepal) and play a major role in maintaining ecosystem stability.

### Core Concepts

- **Habitat Loss & Fragmentation**: Conversion of forests to agriculture and the draining of natural wetlands result in a massive loss of aquatic and terrestrial species.
- **Poaching**: Over-harvesting and illegal hunting driven by large economic benefits.
- **Mass Extinction**: Human actions are drastically increasing the extinction rate (1000 to 10,000 times the natural rate).

### Important Formulas / Relations

Not applicable.

### Procedures / Algorithms

Not applicable.

### Examples

- Marine turtles caught in fishing nets off the coast of Odisha; the rare whale shark being killed off the coast of Gujarat.

### PYQ Importance

- ⭐ **Frequently Asked**: True/False statements regarding whether all invasive species are harmful (they are not).

### Exam Focus

- ⚠️ **Commonly Confused Concept**: Students often assume all foreign species are invasive and destructive. Only a subset becomes invasive.
- 📌 **Must Memorize**: The definition of a Keystone species.

## Topic 3: Biodiversity Conservation Strategies

### Overview

Explores the primary methodologies applied worldwide to prevent species extinction and preserve natural habitats.

### Definitions

- 📝 **In-situ Conservation**: "On-site" conservation; protecting endangered plant or animal species within their natural habitats.
- 📝 **Ex-situ Conservation**: "Off-site" conservation; protecting species outside their natural habitats when extinction is imminent.

### Core Concepts

- **In-situ Methods**: Include National Parks, Wildlife Sanctuaries, and Biosphere Reserves. These areas preserve the recovering populations in their native surroundings.
- **Ex-situ Methods**: Include Zoological parks, Botanical gardens, Seed banks, and Tissue culture. Captive breeding in zoos helps in species recovery for eventual reintroduction.

### Important Formulas / Relations

Not applicable.

### Procedures / Algorithms

Not applicable.

### Examples

- Seed banks utilize natural seed dormancy to suspend preservation for long periods.

### PYQ Importance

- ⭐ **Frequently Asked**: Differentiating between National Parks (In-situ) and Zoological Parks (Ex-situ).

### Exam Focus

- 📌 **Must Memorize**: Seed banks, zoos, and botanical gardens are _Ex-situ_; National Parks and Sanctuaries are _In-situ_.

## Topic 2: Environmental Movements in India

### Overview

A historical look at the grassroots social and political movements in India aimed at resisting environmental destruction and protecting natural resources.

### Definitions

- 📝 **Environmental Movement**: A social movement involving groups and coalitions acting to protect the environment and influence policy.

### Core Concepts

- **Bishnoi Movement**: Originated in Rajasthan. The Bishnoi community plays a fundamental role in environmental and wildlife protection, largely driven by religious and cultural beliefs.
- **Chipko Movement**: Started in 1973 in Uttarakhand by Sunderlal Bahuguna and Gaura Devi; famous for the tree-hugging campaign to resist commercial logging.
- **Save Silent Valley Movement**: Started in 1978 in Kerala to protect an evergreen tropical forest from being destroyed by a hydroelectric project.
- **Narmada Bachao Andolan (NBA)**: Led by Medha Patkar and Baba Amte; centered on human rights and the resettlement/rehabilitation of people displaced by large dam projects.

### Important Formulas / Relations

Not applicable.

### Procedures / Algorithms

Not applicable.

### Examples

- The Appiko movement in Karnataka (1983) which also focused on forest conservation.

### PYQ Importance

- ⭐ **Frequently Asked**: Differentiating the Chipko movement from the Silent Valley movement.
- ⭐ **Frequently Asked**: The specific role of the Bishnoi community in environmental protection.
- ⭐ **Frequently Asked**: True/False identification of key figures (e.g., Gaura Devi is associated with the Chipko movement, _not_ the Bishnoi movement).

### Exam Focus

- ⚠️ **Commonly Confused Concept**: Confusing the leaders of the movements. Gaura Devi = Chipko; Amrita Devi = Bishnois; Medha Patkar = Narmada Bachao.

### Overview

### Definitions

- **Resettlement and Rehabilitation**: Development projects (like dams) cause massive displacement of local communities. Effective post-disaster/project recovery requires minimizing involuntary resettlement and providing time-bound compensation.
